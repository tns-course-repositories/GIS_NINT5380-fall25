Part II - Final Project Components

Your final GIS project is a complete analytical report demonstrating your ability to pose a spatial question, acquire data, conduct GIS analysis, generate maps, and communicate findings clearly.

ğŸ§­ 1. Confirm Your Project Question & Scope

Before writing or mapping, revisit your instructor-reviewed proposal:

  • Is your GIS question clear, specific, and spatial?
  • Have you confirmed datasets and data sources?
  • Is your geographic extent well defined (country, region, city, census tracts, etc.)?
  • Is your scope manageable within the timeline?

Your entire report should directly address your GIS question.

  • Use tables and reports for deliverable materials - outcomes from your GIS workflow(s)

ğŸ§ª 3. Conduct Your Spatial Analysis

From your instructor-reviewed proposal, you should have an initial spatial analysis workflow established.

Your analysis will vary depending on project, but typical components might include:

  • Loading, cleaning, and reproject spatial data
  • Spatial joins & attribute filtering
  • Buffer analysis
  • Overlay & intersection
  • Distance calculations
  • Raster operations
  • Density or cluster analysis
  • Vulnerability model analysis
  • Suitability analysis
  • Change detection over time

  • Spatial formats (vector/raster)
  • Data cleaning and preprocessing
  • CRS and projection details
  • Analysis operations performed (e.g., buffer, intersect, raster analysis, attribute development)
  • Software used (if using any processed outside QGIS)
  • Analytical assumptions or limitations

Present your spatial findings:

  • Maps (with titles, legends, scale bars, north arrows, source citation)
  • Tables, charts, or figures
  • Descriptions of what each mapped or tabular output shows
  • Interpretation of patterns, trends, exposures, risk zones, etc.
  • How results relate directly to your GIS question

This section should be analytical, not merely descriptive.

ğŸ” 4.5 Conclusion

Summarize:

  • The answer to your GIS question
  • The significance of the results
  • Key limitations or uncertainties
  • Future research or recommendations

ğŸ“– 4.6 Works Cited / Bibliography / Data Sources

Three components:

Data Sources

  • dataset title

Software (Optional)

Tip

Use a standard, consistent citation style: APA (American Psychological Association); MLA (Modern Language Association); Chicago Style; Harvard Referencing; IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers).
